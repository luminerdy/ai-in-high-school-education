# STEM Teacher Meeting Outline

Title: AI in Education and in the Workplace

Audience: Local high school STEM teachers

Session length: 60 minutes

Recommended format: 20 minute framing presentation + 35 minute structured discussion + 5 minute close

## Purpose

Help STEM teachers think clearly about how GenAI and agentic AI change student learning, workplace readiness, assignment design, and evidence of understanding.

This should not be a broad "schools should" talk. Target what STEM teachers can directly influence:

- how students use AI
- what thinking remains visible
- what assignments need redesign
- how students verify AI output
- what skills students need for AI-enabled workplaces

## Core Message

AI makes production easier and evaluation harder. The workplace does not need students who can merely get AI to produce output. It needs people who can define tasks, communicate clearly, verify results, notice failure, document decisions, and take responsibility for outcomes.

## Session Flow

| Time | Segment | Goal |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-5 | Opening prompt | Surface the ambiguity of "using AI" |
| 5-20 | Framing presentation | Give shared language and workplace context |
| 20-35 | Small-group assignment discussion | Apply ideas to actual STEM assignments |
| 35-50 | Share-out | Identify common teacher impact areas |
| 50-58 | AI Quest / next-year implications | Connect to competition, camps, mentoring, and classroom practice |
| 58-60 | Close | Name one practical next step |

## Opening Prompt

Ask:

"When someone says a student used AI, what are all the different things that could mean?"

Expected responses:

- copied the answer
- asked for an explanation
- used it as a tutor
- brainstormed ideas
- generated code
- debugged code
- wrote a draft
- edited writing
- summarized research
- made slides
- checked work
- generated images
- planned a project

Bridge:

"That same ambiguity exists in the workplace. 'Do you use AI?' is not the useful question. The useful question is: how, for what task, with what verification, and who is accountable?"

## Presentation Section: 15 Minutes Of Core Content

### 1. AI Use Is Not One Thing

Slide idea:

AI use ladder:

| Level | AI Role | Learning Concern |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Copy answer | Replaces thinking |
| 1 | Q&A | May help or bypass |
| 2 | Prompt engineering | Student directs the tool |
| 3 | Tutor | Student practices with support |
| 4 | Critic/reviewer | Student revises and evaluates |
| 5 | Workflow assistant | Student manages process |
| 6 | Agentic workflow | AI plans/tests/revises with human guardrails |

Key line:

"The question is not whether AI was used. The question is what role AI played in the thinking."

### 2. The Workplace Struggle

Workplaces are wrestling with:

- quality control
- hallucinated or weak output
- privacy and data leakage
- accountability
- tool instability
- overtrust
- uneven skill levels
- unclear rules about what AI should do
- agentic systems that can act, test, and revise without constant human input

Key line:

"As AI gets better at producing work, humans need to get better at defining, judging, explaining, and communicating work."

### 3. What Students Need

Durable skills:

- logic
- critical thinking
- clear writing
- question asking
- evidence use
- domain knowledge
- revision
- communication
- ethical reasoning
- verification
- metacognition

Key line:

"AI raises the floor for producing acceptable-looking work, but it raises the ceiling for students who can think clearly, write clearly, and judge quality."

### 4. STEM Teacher Impact Zone

STEM teachers already teach the skills AI users need:

- models are simplifications
- data quality matters
- assumptions matter
- units matter
- evidence beats confidence
- constraints shape design
- testing reveals failure
- code must be explained and verified

Key line:

"AI makes STEM thinking more important, not less."

### 5. Assignment Evidence Has Changed

Old assumption:

"Finished assignment usually showed learning."

New problem:

"A finished assignment may show production, but not necessarily understanding."

Shift:

From final product only to visible thinking:

- prompt log
- source check
- assumptions
- data
- test cases
- error analysis
- revision notes
- oral defense
- explanation of rejected AI suggestions

Key line:

"GenAI does not make assignments useless. It makes weak assignments easier to fake and strong assignments more important."

## Discussion Activity: 15 Minutes

Have teachers work individually or in small groups.

Prompt:

"Pick one assignment students currently do. How could AI help? How could AI break the learning? What evidence would prove the student still understands?"

Template:

| My Assignment | AI Could Help By | AI Could Break Learning By | Evidence I Need |
|---|---|---|---|
|  |  |  |  |

Examples of evidence:

- annotated prompt log
- rough work
- lab data
- claim-evidence-reasoning
- unit check
- source comparison
- code walkthrough
- test cases
- debugging log
- revision history
- oral explanation
- peer critique
- "what AI got wrong" section

## Share-Out: 15 Minutes

Ask each group to share one assignment and one assessment change.

Capture themes under three headings:

1. Where AI can help learning
2. Where AI can bypass learning
3. What evidence of thinking we need

Useful follow-up questions:

- What student thinking must remain visible?
- What should be AI-allowed, AI-limited, and AI-prohibited?
- What should students have to disclose?
- What would make this manageable for teachers?

## AI Quest / Next-Year Connection: 8 Minutes

Use this if the group is interested in competitions, camps, or enrichment.

Frame:

"AI Quest should not be a contest to see who can produce the best AI output. It should be a contest to see who can use AI with the best human judgment."

Possible competition structure:

| Phase | Student Output |
|---|---|
| Problem framing | goal, constraints, assumptions |
| AI exploration | prompt/use log |
| Build | solution/prototype/plan |
| Verification | tests, source checks, risk checks |
| Revision | what changed and why |
| Defense | short explanation and judge questions |

Judging categories:

- problem framing
- AI use
- verification
- communication
- responsible design

## Regulation And Concerns: Optional Insert

Use only if discussion turns toward safety/regulation.

Short framing:

"Regulation is catching up. Classrooms need guardrails now."

Name concerns:

- privacy
- age appropriateness
- AI companions
- academic integrity
- accuracy
- bias
- disclosure
- equity

Teacher-facing guardrails:

- use approved tools
- do not enter private student data
- require disclosure
- teach verification
- keep some learning moments human-only
- make students responsible for final work

## Closing: 2 Minutes

Ask teachers to write one answer:

"What is one thing you can change next year so students do not just produce better-looking work, but show better thinking?"

Closing line:

"The goal is not to make students dependent on AI, and it is not to pretend AI is going away. The goal is to graduate students who can use powerful tools while remaining accurate, ethical, curious, and responsible for their own thinking."

## Recommended Slide Count

Keep it to 8-10 slides.

1. Title / purpose
2. Opening question: What does "used AI" mean?
3. AI use ladder
4. Workplace struggle
5. Durable student skills
6. STEM teacher impact zone
7. Finished product vs visible thinking
8. Assignment redesign activity
9. AI Quest / enrichment connection
10. Closing question
