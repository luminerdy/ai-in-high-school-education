# Teacher Questions: Where AI Is Going, Ethics, And Detection

Purpose: Prepare concise answers for likely teacher questions during the STEM teacher session.

## Question 1: Where Is AI Going?

Short answer:

AI is moving from answering questions to helping complete workflows. The shift is from chatbot to collaborator to agentic assistant.

Teacher-facing explanation:

Students are not just going to use AI to write paragraphs. They will increasingly use AI to:

- brainstorm
- explain
- summarize
- write
- code
- debug
- plan
- test
- revise
- generate visuals
- search across documents
- operate tools
- coordinate multi-step work

The workplace issue is not "Can people use AI?" It is "Can people use AI responsibly inside real work?"

Key line:

The future is not just better answers. It is AI embedded in workflows.

## Question 2: What Are The Ethics Around Assignments?

Short answer:

Ethics depend on the role AI plays in the work. AI use is not one thing.

Use this distinction:

| AI Role | Ethical Concern | Likely Classroom Policy |
|---|---|---|
| AI gives final answer | Replaces student thinking | Usually prohibited |
| AI explains a concept | Can support learning | Often allowed |
| AI brainstorms ideas | Can help starting | Usually allowed with disclosure |
| AI edits writing | May improve communication or hide ability | Limited / disclosed |
| AI critiques student work | Supports revision | Often useful |
| AI generates code | May bypass skill development | Depends on assignment goal |
| AI checks work | Useful if student verifies | Allowed with evidence |
| AI manages a workflow | Powerful but needs guardrails | Advanced / documented |

The ethical question is not just "Did the student use AI?"

Better questions:

- What role did AI play?
- Did AI replace the learning target?
- Did the student disclose use?
- Can the student explain the final work?
- Did the student verify output?
- Was private or protected information used?
- Did the student submit AI output as original thinking?

Key line:

AI use is ethical when it supports learning, is transparent, and the student remains responsible for the final work.

## Question 3: Can We Detect AI In Assignments?

Short answer:

Not reliably enough to use detection alone as proof of misconduct.

Current detection problems:

- false positives can accuse honest students
- non-native English writers can be disproportionately flagged
- human-edited AI text is harder to detect
- paraphrasing and "humanizer" tools can evade detectors
- different detectors often disagree
- detection gets harder as models improve
- detector scores do not tell what role AI played

Useful framing:

AI detectors can be a signal for a conversation, not a verdict.

Better evidence:

- drafting history
- process notes
- prompt/use logs
- source notes
- oral explanation
- in-class writing or problem solving
- version history
- code walkthrough
- lab notebook
- test cases
- comparison of student reasoning to final output

Key line:

The assessment problem is not only detection. It is learning visibility.

## Suggested Slide

Title: The Three Questions Teachers Are Asking

| Question | Practical Answer |
|---|---|
| Where is AI going? | From answers to workflows and agents |
| What is ethical assignment use? | Depends on the role AI plays and whether thinking remains visible |
| Can we detect AI? | Not reliably enough for proof; design for evidence of learning |

## Suggested Spoken Answer

"The short version is: AI is moving from answer generation to workflow support. That means assignment ethics cannot be handled with a simple yes/no AI rule. We need to ask what role AI played. Did it tutor, brainstorm, critique, test, or produce the final answer? For detection, the evidence is clear enough that we should be cautious. AI detectors can raise a question, but they should not be the sole evidence for accusing a student. A better approach is to design assignments where students show process, verification, and explanation."

## Practical Teacher Move

Use assignment labels:

### AI-Prohibited

Use when the goal is independent skill practice or assessment.

Examples:

- in-class math check
- first draft diagnostic
- safety-critical lab decision
- basic coding syntax practice

### AI-Limited

Use when AI may support but not replace the work.

Examples:

- brainstorming
- explaining
- outlining
- feedback
- debugging hints
- revision suggestions

Require disclosure.

### AI-Allowed

Use when the goal includes AI workflow literacy.

Examples:

- capstone planning
- design review
- test-case generation
- AI critique assignment
- project documentation
- comparison of AI and human reasoning

Require process evidence.

## Discussion Prompt

"Pick an assignment. Is it AI-prohibited, AI-limited, or AI-allowed? What evidence would you need to trust the learning?"
