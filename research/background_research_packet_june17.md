Date: June 17, 2026

Audience: Local high school STEM teachers

## Working Thesis

AI is becoming a general-purpose workplace capability. For students, the important skill is not simply "using AI." The important skill is using AI inside a real workflow while preserving human judgment, privacy, verification, communication, and accountability.

For STEM teachers, the practical instructional question is:

How do we let AI help students learn and build while still making student thinking visible?

## Current State Of High School GenAI

### Student Use

Common Sense Media's 2024 report found that seven in ten U.S. teens had used at least one type of generative AI tool, and that students were using AI most often for schoolwork. The report also emphasized a communication gap between students, parents, and schools.

Use this carefully:

- This is broad GenAI use, not only ChatGPT.
- It includes school and non-school uses.
- It supports the claim that AI use is already normalizing among teens.

Source:

- Common Sense Media, "The Dawn of the AI Era: Teens, Parents, and the Adoption of Generative AI at Home and School," September 18, 2024. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/the-dawn-of-the-ai-era-teens-parents-and-the-adoption-of-generative-ai-at-home-and-school

Pew's 2024 teen data, widely reported in January 2025, found that 26% of teens ages 13-17 said they had used ChatGPT for schoolwork, up from 13% in 2023. This is narrower than the Common Sense figure because it asks about ChatGPT specifically and schoolwork specifically. Treat this as a supporting point unless the original Pew page is available for direct citation.

Use this carefully:

- Do not compare 70% and 26% as if they measure the same thing.
- One is any GenAI use; the other is ChatGPT for schoolwork.
- The Common Sense link is the primary source for broad teen GenAI use; the Pew statistic should be cited through Pew directly if the source page is available, otherwise use secondary coverage with caution.

### Teacher And Principal Use

RAND's nationally representative survey from the 2023-2024 school year found:

- 25% of K-12 teachers used AI tools for instructional planning or teaching.
- Nearly 60% of principals used AI tools for their work.
- ELA and science teachers were nearly twice as likely to use AI as math teachers or elementary teachers.
- Only 18% of principals reported that their school or district had provided AI guidance.
- High-poverty schools were less likely to report guidance than low-poverty schools.

Presentation use:

Teachers are already experimenting, but guidance and training are uneven.

Source:

- RAND, "Uneven Adoption of Artificial Intelligence Tools Among U.S. Teachers and Principals in the 2023-2024 School Year," February 11, 2025. https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA134-25.html

## Guidance And Policy Landscape

TeachAI's 2025 AI Guidance for Schools Toolkit is one of the most useful teacher-facing guidance sources. It emphasizes:

- developing a vision
- reviewing existing academic integrity, privacy, and responsible-use policies
- integrating AI literacy into professional development and curriculum
- communicating with parents, staff, and students
- revising guidance over time

TeachAI specifically notes that teachers can use the toolkit to inform their use of AI in instruction and assessment and how students should or should not use AI when completing assignments.

Source:

- TeachAI, "AI Guidance for Schools Toolkit," 2025. https://www.teachai.org/toolkit

The U.S. Department of Education's 2023 report remains useful for broad principles:

- AI should support, not replace, human judgment.
- Risks include bias, privacy, surveillance, inaccurate output, and over-automation.
- Humans should remain meaningfully involved in decisions about teaching and learning.

Source:

- U.S. Department of Education, Office of Educational Technology, "Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning," May 2023. https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf

The April 2025 White House executive order on AI education framed AI literacy and proficiency as a national education and workforce priority, including teacher training, public-private partnerships, high school AI courses, certifications, and work-based learning.

Source:

- White House, "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth," April 23, 2025. https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/04/advancing-artificial-intelligence-education-for-american-youth/

## Workplace Readiness

### What Employers Need

The useful workplace framing is not:

"Does the candidate use AI?"

It is:

"Can the candidate use AI inside a real workflow while protecting quality, privacy, accuracy, and accountability?"

Candidate capabilities:

| Capability | Workplace Meaning | High School Translation |
|---|---|---|
| Problem framing | Define goal, audience, constraints, success criteria | State goal, assumptions, and what good work requires |
| Clear communication | Give context and explain results | Write precise prompts and clear explanations |
| Domain knowledge | Spot weak or false AI output | Know enough STEM to challenge AI |
| Verification | Check sources, data, tests, calculations | Cite evidence, check units, run tests |
| Revision judgment | Accept/reject/modify suggestions | Explain what changed and why |
| Privacy/safety | Avoid sensitive data in unapproved tools | Know what not to enter into AI |
| Accountability | Own final output | Defend final answer |
| Ethical reasoning | Consider bias, fairness, harm | Identify affected people and failure modes |
| Adaptability | Learn new tools without tool dependency | Build transferable AI literacy |

## Assignment Ethics

The ethical issue is not "AI or no AI." It is the role AI plays.

| AI Role | Ethical Concern | Likely Classroom Policy |
|---|---|---|
| Final answer generator | Replaces student thinking | Usually prohibited |
| Explainer | Can support learning | Often allowed |
| Brainstorming partner | Can help start | Usually allowed with disclosure |
| Editor | May improve or hide skill gaps | Limited/disclosed |
| Critic/reviewer | Supports revision | Often useful |
| Code generator | May bypass skill development | Depends on assignment goal |
| Checker/tester | Useful if student verifies | Allowed with evidence |
| Workflow assistant | Powerful but needs guardrails | Advanced/documented |

Recommended teacher question:

"What role did AI play, and what student thinking remains visible?"

## AI Detection

Do not rely on AI detectors as the main answer.

Current issues:

- false positives can accuse honest students
- non-native English writers can be disproportionately flagged
- human-edited AI text is harder to detect
- paraphrasing/humanizer tools can evade detection
- different detectors disagree
- detection scores do not explain what role AI played

Recommended stance:

AI detectors can be a signal for a conversation, not a verdict.

Better evidence:

- drafting history
- process notes
- prompt/use logs
- source notes
- oral explanation
- in-class checks
- version history
- code walkthroughs
- lab notebook
- test cases
- comparison of student reasoning to final output

Sources:

- Liang et al., "GPT detectors are biased against non-native English writers," 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.02819
- Gehring and Paassen, "Assessing LLM Text Detection in Educational Contexts," 2025. https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.08096
- Davalos and Zhang, "AI Misuse in Education Is a Measurement Problem," 2026. https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.07834
- Garland, "AI Detectors Fail Diverse Student Populations," 2026. https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.20254

## Capstones And Projects

Capstone and project-based learning may be the best constructive AI use case for STEM.

AI can help students:

- scope a project
- identify requirements
- find parts/tools
- install software
- configure hardware
- understand errors
- debug code
- read documentation
- plan tests
- write documentation

Student still owns:

- project goal
- design choices
- safety
- verification
- final explanation
- working prototype or evidence

Recommended line:

AI can lower the barrier to starting. The teacher's job is to make sure it does not lower the requirement to understand.

## Regulation, Privacy, And Youth Safety

The U.S. does not have one comprehensive federal "AI for children" law. The current landscape is a patchwork:

- COPPA for online data collection from children under 13
- FERPA for student education records
- FTC consumer protection
- state AI, privacy, deepfake, and chatbot laws
- district acceptable-use and academic-integrity policies

For teachers:

- use approved tools
- do not enter private student data into unapproved AI tools
- require disclosure when AI supports schoolwork
- teach verification
- keep some learning moments human-only
- avoid AI companion-style tools unless reviewed and approved

AI companions deserve separate treatment from learning tools. A practice tutor is not the same risk profile as a bot designed to simulate friendship, romance, emotional support, or personal advice.

Sources:

- TeachAI toolkit: https://www.teachai.org/toolkit
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework: https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework
- NIST Generative AI Profile: https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.600-1
- AP coverage of FTC inquiry into AI companion chatbots and children/teens: https://apnews.com/article/e78fcc72520f56a4eff90df7ad6220c0

## Practical Teacher Framework

### AI-Prohibited

Use when the learning goal is independent performance.

Examples:

- in-class checks
- first-draft diagnostic
- basic math procedure practice
- safety-critical lab decisions
- basic syntax practice

### AI-Limited

Use when AI may support but not replace the learning target.

Examples:

- brainstorming
- explanation
- outlining
- feedback
- debugging hints
- revision suggestions

Requirement:

Disclosure and evidence of student thinking.

### AI-Allowed

Use when the goal includes AI workflow literacy.

Examples:

- capstone planning
- design review
- test-case generation
- AI critique assignments
- project documentation
- comparison of AI and human reasoning

Requirement:

Process evidence, verification, and explanation.

## Best Teacher-Facing Summary

The question is not:

"Did you use AI?"

The better questions are:

- What role did AI play?
- What thinking did the student do?
- What evidence shows the result is reliable?
- Can the student explain and defend the work?
