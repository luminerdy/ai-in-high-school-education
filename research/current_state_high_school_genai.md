# Current State of High School GenAI Education

Date reviewed: June 12, 2026

Focus: U.S. high school education, especially generative AI use by students and teachers.

## Bottom Line

U.S. high schools are moving from "ban or ignore GenAI" toward "governed integration," but implementation is uneven. Students are already using GenAI, teachers are increasingly using it for planning and productivity, and states/districts are writing guidance. The biggest gap is not awareness; it is clear, practical rules for when GenAI use supports learning, when it bypasses learning, and how students should disclose and verify AI-assisted work.

## Student Use

The available data varies depending on what is being measured.

Common Sense Media's 2024 high school survey, as reported by Wired, found that about 70% of U.S. teenagers had used at least one generative AI tool. The most common school-related use was homework help, especially brainstorming. Wired also reported that more than 80% of parents said their child's school had not communicated anything about generative AI.

Pew Research Center's 2024 teen data, as reported by multiple outlets, found that 26% of teens ages 13-17 said they had used ChatGPT for schoolwork, up from 13% in 2023. This is narrower than the Common Sense finding because it asks about ChatGPT specifically and schoolwork specifically, not any GenAI use. Use this as a supporting data point unless the original Pew page is available for direct citation.

Interpretation for the presentation:

- A large share of high school students have at least experimented with GenAI.
- A smaller but fast-growing share say they use ChatGPT specifically for schoolwork.
- Students are using GenAI for both acceptable and questionable purposes: brainstorming, explanation, summarizing, translation, writing help, math help, coding help, and sometimes unauthorized completion of assignments.
- Many students do not know the rules, or the rules vary by teacher.

## Teacher Use

RAND's nationally representative survey of U.S. K-12 teachers and principals during the 2023-2024 school year found:

- 25% of surveyed teachers used AI tools for instructional planning or teaching.
- Nearly 60% of principals used AI tools for their work.
- ELA and science teachers were nearly twice as likely to report using AI as math teachers or elementary teachers.
- 18% of principals said their schools or districts had provided guidance on AI use.
- Highest-poverty schools were less likely to report guidance than lowest-poverty schools, 13% vs. 25%.

Teacher-facing GenAI use is mostly productivity-oriented:

- Lesson planning
- Generating examples or explanations
- Differentiating reading levels
- Writing rubrics
- Drafting parent communications
- Generating practice problems
- Creating quiz questions
- Brainstorming activities
- Providing first-draft feedback

Interpretation for the presentation:

Teachers are often adopting GenAI faster for their own workflow than for student-facing assignments. This makes sense: teacher-facing use can save time without immediately raising the same academic integrity and privacy issues as student use.

## Policy And Guidance

TeachAI's 2025 toolkit says many education systems still face an urgent need for clear, structured AI guidance. The toolkit recommends updating or developing guidance around:

- Foundational principles
- Existing academic integrity, privacy, and responsible-use policies
- AI literacy in professional development and curriculum
- Ongoing feedback and revision
- Parent, staff, and student communication

TeachAI's policy tracker lists 30 U.S. state AI education guidance entries as of review on June 12, 2026. The tracker also includes guidance from several countries and the U.S. Department of Education.

Interpretation for the presentation:

- Guidance is expanding, but unevenly.
- Districts are increasingly moving from simple bans toward categories such as allowed, limited, and prohibited use.
- Many policies still put too much burden on individual teachers.

## Curriculum And Courses

AI education is emerging in three forms:

1. Standalone AI courses
   - Example: PLTW's Principles of Artificial Intelligence course information for AY26-27.
   - High-level themes include responsible use, prompt/context engineering, data literacy, model behavior, bias, human-in-the-loop thinking, automation risks, and AI-supported problem solving.

2. AI literacy modules inside existing courses
   - STEM, English, social studies, computer science, and CTE courses can include AI critique, source verification, AI-assisted drafting, data analysis, and ethical reasoning.

3. Teacher professional learning
   - Many schools are starting with teacher AI literacy before approving broad student use.

Interpretation for the presentation:

The best high school AI education is not simply "teach prompt engineering." It combines:

- Functional use: how to use tools productively
- Critical use: how to verify, critique, and recognize bias
- Technical understanding: how data, models, and probability shape output
- Ethical use: privacy, attribution, fairness, and human responsibility

## Main Tensions

### Academic Integrity

Schools are still struggling to define what counts as cheating versus acceptable assistance. AI detection tools are not a complete solution and can create fairness concerns, especially if used as the main evidence in accusations.

### Learning Versus Completion

GenAI can help students brainstorm, study, and receive feedback. It can also let students bypass productive struggle. The assignment design matters more than the tool itself.

### Privacy And Age Limits

Many general-purpose GenAI tools were not designed for minors or school compliance. Schools need approved tools, data rules, and clear parent communication.

### Equity

Equity now includes access to guidance and teacher support, not just access to devices. RAND found high-poverty schools had less AI guidance.

### Teacher Capacity

Teachers need examples, time, approved tools, and shared norms. "Figure it out yourself" is not a serious implementation strategy.

## Good Presentation Framing

Use this phrase:

"The current state of high school GenAI education is not settled adoption. It is fast, uneven normalization."

Or:

"Students are already using GenAI. Teachers are experimenting. Guidance is catching up. Assessment has to change."

## Suggested Slide

Title: Current State of GenAI in High School

Slide bullets:

- Students are already using it, often without clear rules.
- Teachers are using it mostly for planning, materials, feedback, and productivity.
- States and districts are moving from bans to guardrails.
- Standalone AI courses are emerging, but most schools are still embedding AI literacy into existing classes.
- The unresolved challenge is assessment: how students show thinking when AI can generate polished output.
