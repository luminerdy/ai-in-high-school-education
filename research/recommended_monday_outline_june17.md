# Recommended Monday Outline: STEM Teachers And AI

Date: June 17, 2026

Meeting date: Monday, June 22, 2026

Session length: 60 minutes

Recommended format: 18-20 minute presentation, 35 minute teacher discussion, 5 minute close.

## Session Goal

Give STEM teachers a practical framework for deciding how AI should be used in assignments, projects, capstones, and student skill development.

The goal is not to answer every AI policy question. The goal is to help teachers decide what student thinking must remain visible when AI is available.

## Core Message

AI makes production easier and evaluation harder. STEM teachers can respond by teaching students to frame problems, verify output, document process, communicate clearly, and defend decisions.

## Slide 1: Title And Purpose

Title:

AI in Education and the Workplace: Practical Guidance for STEM Teachers

Purpose:

What can STEM teachers do next year so AI supports learning instead of hiding it?

Speaker note:

Do not start with a long definition of AI. Start with the classroom problem.

## Slide 2: Opening Question

Ask:

"When someone says a student used AI, what could that mean?"

Let teachers answer.

Expected answers:

- copied answer
- asked for explanation
- brainstormed
- edited writing
- debugged code
- generated code
- summarized research
- made slides
- checked work
- planned a project

Bridge:

"That same ambiguity exists in the workplace. 'Do you use AI?' is not the useful question. The useful question is: how, for what task, with what verification, and who is accountable?"

## Slide 3: AI Use Ladder

| Level | AI Role | Learning Concern |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Copy answer | Replaces thinking |
| 1 | Q&A | May help or bypass |
| 2 | Prompt/context engineering | Student directs the tool |
| 3 | Tutor | Student practices with support |
| 4 | Critic/reviewer | Student revises and evaluates |
| 5 | Workflow assistant | Student manages process |
| 6 | Agentic workflow | AI plans/tests/revises under human guardrails |

Key line:

The question is not whether AI was used. The question is what role AI played in the thinking.

## Slide 4: Where AI Is Going

Main idea:

AI is moving from answers to workflows.

Include:

- text, code, images, audio, video, data, and tools
- planning, testing, revising, and acting
- agents that can complete steps with less human prompting

Teacher translation:

Students need to learn supervision, verification, and accountability, not just prompting.

## Slide 5: Workplace Need

Title:

Employers Need AI Judgment, Not Vague AI Use

Candidate capabilities:

- problem framing
- clear communication
- domain knowledge
- verification
- revision judgment
- privacy and safety
- accountability
- ethical reasoning
- adaptability

Key line:

The workplace does not need students who can make AI produce a nice answer. It needs people who can define the problem, use AI appropriately, verify the result, communicate clearly, and own the decision.

## Slide 6: What STEM Teachers Already Teach

Title:

AI Makes STEM Thinking More Important

STEM habits:

- models are simplifications
- data quality matters
- assumptions matter
- units matter
- evidence beats confidence
- constraints shape design
- testing reveals failure
- code must be explained and verified

Key line:

AI often fails in exactly the places STEM teachers already care about.

## Slide 7: Assignment Evidence Has Changed

Old assumption:

Finished assignment usually showed learning.

New problem:

Finished work may show production, but not understanding.

Shift:

From product only to visible thinking.

Examples of visible thinking:

- prompt/use log
- source check
- assumptions
- rough work
- lab data
- test cases
- error analysis
- revision notes
- oral defense
- rejected AI suggestions

Key line:

GenAI does not make assignments useless. It makes weak assignments easier to fake and strong assignments more important.

## Slide 8: Ethics And Detection

Title:

Ethics Depends On AI's Role. Detection Is Not Enough.

Three points:

1. AI use can be ethical when it supports learning, is disclosed, and the student remains responsible.
2. AI use is a problem when it replaces the learning target or hides the student's actual understanding.
3. AI detectors can raise a question, but should not be treated as proof.

Key line:

The assessment problem is not only detection. It is learning visibility.

## Slide 9: Practical Assignment Categories

| Category | Use When | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| AI-Prohibited | independent skill practice or assessment | no AI |
| AI-Limited | AI can support but not replace learning | disclose use |
| AI-Allowed | AI workflow literacy is part of the goal | process evidence and verification |

Examples:

- Prohibited: in-class check, first-draft diagnostic, safety-critical lab decision.
- Limited: brainstorming, explanation, feedback, debugging hints.
- Allowed: capstone planning, design review, test-case generation, project documentation.

## Slide 10: Capstones And Projects

Title:

Capstones: AI Can Reduce Friction Without Replacing Learning

AI can help with:

- idea scope
- requirements
- setup
- configuration
- debugging
- documentation
- test planning
- presentation structure

Student still owns:

- design choices
- safety
- verification
- prototype evidence
- final explanation

Key line:

AI can lower the barrier to starting. The teacher's job is to make sure it does not lower the requirement to understand.

## Activity: Assignment Redesign Discussion

Time: 15 minutes small group, 15 minutes share-out.

Prompt:

"Pick one assignment or project students currently do. How could AI help? How could AI break the learning? What evidence would prove the student still understands?"

Template:

| Assignment | AI Could Help By | AI Could Break Learning By | Evidence I Need |
|---|---|---|---|
|  |  |  |  |

## Share-Out Questions

Ask:

- What student thinking must remain visible?
- What should be AI-prohibited, AI-limited, or AI-allowed?
- What should students disclose?
- What evidence would make assessment manageable?
- What would you change in one assignment next year?

## Optional Insert If Regulation Comes Up

Use a short answer only:

"Regulation is catching up. Classrooms need guardrails now."

Guardrails:

- approved tools
- no private student data in unapproved tools
- disclosure
- verification
- human-only moments
- students own final work
- caution with AI companions

## Closing

Ask:

"What is one assignment or project you can adjust next year so students show their thinking, not just their output?"

Final line:

The goal is not to make students dependent on AI, and it is not to pretend AI is going away. The goal is to help students use powerful tools while remaining accurate, ethical, and responsible for their own thinking.
