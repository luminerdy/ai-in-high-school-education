# AI in Education and the Workplace: Research Brief

Audience: Local high school STEM teachers, with possible relevance for middle school.

Working title: "AI in Education and in the Workplace"

## Draft Thesis

AI is not just another edtech tool. It is becoming a general-purpose workplace capability that changes how people write, code, analyze, design, research, communicate, and make decisions. Schools should prepare students not merely to use AI tools, but to understand AI systems, evaluate their outputs, protect privacy, recognize bias, document their process, and decide when human judgment matters most.

For STEM teachers, the practical question is:

How do we help students use AI as a thinking partner without letting it replace the thinking students need to develop?

## High-Level Findings

### 1. Federal policy now treats AI literacy as a national education and workforce priority.

In April 2025, the White House issued the executive order "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth." The order sets a federal policy to promote AI literacy and proficiency, integrate AI appropriately into education, train educators, and foster early exposure to AI concepts to build an AI-ready workforce.

Presentation use:

- This is useful as a "why now" source.
- It connects K-12 education directly to workforce development.
- It specifically calls out teacher training, public-private partnerships, high school AI courses, certifications, work-based learning, and AI-related apprenticeships.

Source:

- White House. "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth." April 23, 2025. https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/04/advancing-artificial-intelligence-education-for-american-youth/

### 2. The U.S. Department of Education's strongest guidance is still "human in the loop."

The U.S. Department of Education's 2023 report remains one of the most useful federal sources for educator audiences. It argues that AI in education should be governed because AI can detect patterns in data and automate decisions about instruction and educational processes. It emphasizes risks around bias, privacy, surveillance, inaccurate output, academic integrity, and automated decisions at scale.

The report's recurring message: AI should support teachers and learners, not replace human professional judgment.

Presentation use:

- Use for "AI should augment, not replace, teachers."
- Use for risks: bias, privacy, transparency, explainability, surveillance, and over-automation.
- Use for a teacher-facing principle: AI systems should be inspectable, explainable, and overridable.

Source:

- U.S. Department of Education, Office of Educational Technology. "Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning: Insights and Recommendations." May 2023. https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf

### 3. Teacher use is happening, but guidance is uneven and inequitable.

RAND's nationally representative survey from the 2023-2024 school year found:

- 25% of surveyed K-12 teachers used AI tools for instructional planning or teaching.
- Nearly 60% of principals used AI tools for their work.
- ELA and science teachers were nearly twice as likely to use AI as math teachers or elementary teachers.
- 18% of principals reported that their school or district provided AI guidance.
- Highest-poverty schools were less likely to report guidance than lowest-poverty schools, 13% vs. 25%.

Presentation use:

- This is a strong "teachers are already experimenting, but systems have not caught up" slide.
- For STEM teachers, note that science teachers reported higher use than math teachers in this survey.
- The poverty gap is important: without guidance, AI may widen inequities.

Source:

- Kaufman, Julia H., Ashley Woo, Joshua Eagan, Sabrina Lee, and Emma B. Kassan. "Uneven Adoption of Artificial Intelligence Tools Among U.S. Teachers and Principals in the 2023-2024 School Year." RAND Corporation, February 11, 2025. https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA134-25.html

### 4. School systems are moving from bans toward guidance, but the landscape is still fragmented.

TeachAI's 2025 toolkit is designed for education authorities, school leaders, and teachers creating AI guidance for primary and secondary education. It recommends developing a vision, adopting principles, reviewing existing policies, integrating AI literacy into professional development and curriculum, and gathering ongoing feedback.

TeachAI's tracker listed 30 U.S. states with AI education guidance as of the page reviewed on June 12, 2026. The toolkit also notes that only 26 U.S. states had issued guidance as of April 2025, showing rapid but incomplete growth.

Presentation use:

- Use to show that "no guidance" is no longer a sustainable position.
- Use as a source for practical categories: academic integrity, privacy, responsible use, AI literacy, parent/student communication.
- Use to recommend that local policy should define allowed, limited, and prohibited AI uses.

Sources:

- TeachAI. "AI Guidance for Schools Toolkit." 2025. https://www.teachai.org/toolkit
- TeachAI. "AI in Education Policy and Guidance Tracker." https://www.teachai.org/policy-tracker

### 5. PLTW provides a concrete high school model for AI literacy.

PLTW's Principles of Artificial Intelligence course information provides a useful high-level example of AI literacy as a hands-on, project-based high school topic. It appears to emphasize responsible AI use, academic use, prompt and context engineering, data literacy, model behavior, bias and fairness, human-in-the-loop thinking, automation risks, and AI-supported problem solving.

The course information also points toward workplace-relevant skills such as data analysis, visualization, communication, documentation, teamwork, and ethical reasoning.

Presentation use:

- Use as a concrete "what AI education could look like" example.
- Use to argue that AI readiness is interdisciplinary and project-based.
- Use to connect AI to STEM practices: data, models, algorithms, systems, iteration, evidence, documentation.

Sources:

- PLTW, "Principles of Artificial Intelligence" course information, 2026. See PLTW for official course details.
- Local summary notes: `research/pltw_principles_of_ai_notes.md`

### 6. Employers expect AI to transform skills, but human skills rise in importance.

The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report surveyed more than 1,000 global employers representing over 14 million workers. It found:

- 86% of employers expect AI and information processing technologies to transform their business by 2030.
- AI and big data, networks and cybersecurity, and technological literacy are among the fastest-growing skills.
- Analytical thinking remains the most sought-after core skill, with seven in ten companies considering it essential in 2025.
- 39% of workers' existing skill sets are expected to transform or become outdated by 2030.
- 63% of employers identify skill gaps as a major barrier to business transformation.
- Half of employers plan to reorient their business in response to AI, two-thirds plan to hire talent with specific AI skills, and 40% anticipate reducing staff where AI can automate tasks.

Presentation use:

- Use to avoid hype and ground the workplace section in employer expectations.
- Pair technical skills with durable human skills: analytical thinking, resilience, flexibility, creativity, lifelong learning, leadership, and social influence.
- For STEM teachers: the students' advantage will not be "they can ask ChatGPT a question"; it will be that they can frame, test, evaluate, and communicate.

Source:

- World Economic Forum. "The Future of Jobs Report 2025." January 7, 2025. https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/

### 7. The emerging workplace skill is not simple prompting; it is directing, reviewing, and owning AI-assisted work.

Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index analyzed anonymized Microsoft 365 signals and surveyed 20,000 AI-using workers across 10 countries. Microsoft reports that:

- 49% of sampled Copilot conversations supported cognitive work such as analyzing information, solving problems, evaluating, and thinking creatively.
- 66% of AI users surveyed said AI allowed them to spend more time on high-value work.
- 86% said they treat AI output as a starting point rather than a final answer and remain responsible for the thinking.
- Users named quality control of AI output and critical thinking as top human skills as AI takes on more work.
- Only 26% of AI users said leadership is clearly aligned on AI.
- Organizational factors such as culture, manager support, and talent practices accounted for more than twice the reported AI impact of individual factors.

Presentation use:

- Translate this into a student skill set: set intent, ask good questions, evaluate outputs, verify evidence, revise, document, and take responsibility.
- Use "AI output is a first draft, not a final answer" as a classroom norm.
- Make the workplace connection: future workers may be expected to supervise AI-generated work, not just produce everything manually.

Source:

- Microsoft WorkLab. "2026 Work Trend Index Annual Report: Agents, human agency, and the opportunity for every organization." May 5, 2026. https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index/agents-human-agency-and-the-opportunity-for-every-organization

## Presentation-Ready Claims

### Claim A: AI literacy belongs in STEM, but it is bigger than computer science.

Evidence:

- PLTW course information points toward data literacy, model behavior, bias, academic use, prompt/context engineering, and human-centered workflows.
- The White House EO explicitly calls for AI skills across K-12, workforce development, apprenticeships, certifications, and high school opportunities.
- WEF identifies AI/big data and technological literacy as fast-growing skills, but also emphasizes analytical thinking and resilience.

Possible slide title:

"AI literacy is not just prompting. It is data, models, judgment, ethics, and communication."

### Claim B: Students need domain knowledge more, not less.

Evidence:

- AI systems can produce incorrect or inappropriate output, and the Department of Education warns of risks around inaccurate output and automated decisions.
- Microsoft found users themselves identify quality control and critical thinking as top human skills when using AI.
- PLTW course information points toward evaluation of AI output, responsible collaboration with AI, and documentation.

Possible slide title:

"The better the AI gets, the more students need to know enough to challenge it."

### Claim C: The workplace is shifting from producing first drafts to evaluating systems and outcomes.

Evidence:

- Microsoft describes human work shifting toward setting intent, directing work, evaluating outputs, and owning outcomes.
- WEF projects broad skill transformation and strong demand for analytical thinking, AI/big data, technological literacy, creativity, and lifelong learning.

Possible slide title:

"Future-ready students will not just use AI. They will supervise it."

### Claim D: Schools need clear classroom norms now.

Evidence:

- RAND found only 18% of principals reported AI guidance during 2023-2024.
- TeachAI recommends updating responsible use, academic integrity, privacy, and communication policies.
- Student use is widespread enough that "ignore it" is not a classroom strategy.

Possible slide title:

"No policy is still a policy. It just leaves teachers and students guessing."

### Claim E: The equity issue is not only access to AI tools; it is access to guidance.

Evidence:

- RAND found high-poverty schools had lower rates of AI guidance than lower-poverty schools.
- TeachAI's toolkit frames AI guidance as a system-level need for safe, equitable adoption.
- The Department of Education emphasizes equity, transparency, safety, privacy, and explainability.

Possible slide title:

"AI can widen gaps when support is uneven."

## Suggested Teacher-Facing Framework

Use this as a simple classroom model:

### Use AI to Extend Thinking

Good uses:

- Brainstorming questions or possible approaches
- Explaining a concept in multiple ways
- Generating practice problems
- Checking code or finding likely bugs
- Creating first-pass data visualizations
- Simulating a stakeholder or reviewer
- Comparing alternative explanations

### Require Human Proof of Thinking

Student evidence:

- Show the original prompt and revised prompt
- Identify what the AI got wrong or missed
- Cite non-AI sources
- Explain reasoning orally or in writing
- Submit process notes, data, code, or revision history
- Reflect on when AI helped and when it hurt

### Keep Human-Only Moments

Examples:

- Initial struggle with a core concept
- In-class assessment
- Lab safety decisions
- Personal reflection
- Oral defense
- Final judgment about evidence
- Work involving private or sensitive information

## STEM Classroom Examples To Develop

### Biology or Environmental Science

Students use AI to propose possible explanations for a local environmental dataset, then verify claims against real data and credible sources. Final product must include "AI suggested / evidence supported / evidence contradicted."

### Chemistry

Students ask AI to explain a reaction mechanism or lab result, then critique the explanation for missing assumptions, incorrect terms, or unsafe lab advice.

### Physics

Students use AI to generate multiple solution strategies for a mechanics problem, then compare which strategy best maps to the diagram, known variables, and units.

### Engineering

Students use AI to brainstorm design constraints and failure modes, then build a human-reviewed risk table and prototype test plan.

### Computer Science

Students use AI to review code, then must explain every suggested change, reject at least one weak suggestion, and document final reasoning.

### Math

Students compare AI-generated worked solutions to a teacher solution and identify incorrect assumptions, skipped steps, and alternate valid strategies.

## Provisional One-Sentence Close

The goal is not to make students dependent on AI, and it is not to pretend AI is going away; the goal is to graduate students who can use powerful tools while remaining accurate, ethical, curious, and responsible for their own thinking.
