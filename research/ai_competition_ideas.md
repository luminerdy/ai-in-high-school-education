# AI Competition Ideas

Purpose: Capture ideas for a 3-hour, in-person student AI competition that can be judged quickly while still assessing student thinking.

## Core Idea

Run the competition as an AI build sprint:

> Students use AI to build something useful, but they are judged on problem framing, AI workflow, verification, communication, and ownership.

The event should not be judged mainly on the most polished final app or output. A polished result may hide weak understanding. The better goal is to reward students who can use AI with strong human judgment.

## Competition Goals

Students should demonstrate that they can:

- define a problem clearly
- use AI intentionally
- build or improve a useful artifact
- verify AI-supported output
- explain decisions and tradeoffs
- identify limitations and risks
- take responsibility for the final result

This aligns well with workplace AI expectations: people need to define, supervise, verify, communicate, and own AI-supported work.

## Possible Competition Formats

### Option A: AI Build Sprint

Teams receive a challenge prompt at the start and have 3 hours to create a small prototype or solution using approved AI tools.

Possible outputs:

- simple web app
- chatbot or assistant prototype
- data analysis or dashboard
- automation workflow
- classroom or STEM learning tool
- Raspberry Pi / robotics support concept
- low-code or no-code app mockup
- agentic workflow plan with tested steps

Best when the goal is creativity, energy, and broad participation.

### Option B: Fix The Broken AI

Teams receive a flawed AI-generated answer, app, dataset, plan, or explanation. They must identify problems, verify facts, improve the result, and explain what changed.

Best when the goal is quick judging and strong assessment of critical thinking.

This format directly tests whether students can detect weak AI output rather than being impressed by it.

### Option C: Build From A Flawed AI Starting Point

Teams receive both a challenge and an imperfect AI-generated starting point. Their job is to improve it into a useful prototype or plan.

This may be the strongest format because it combines:

- creativity
- debugging
- verification
- AI literacy
- communication
- ownership

## Possible Challenge Themes

### STEM Learning Assistant

Create an AI-supported tool that helps a student learn a STEM concept without simply giving answers.

Assessment angle:

- Does the tool promote learning?
- Does it ask questions or guide thinking?
- Does it avoid just giving away final answers?

### Lab Or Data Helper

Create a tool that helps collect, clean, graph, explain, or check experimental data.

Assessment angle:

- Does it handle data carefully?
- Are assumptions clear?
- Are graphs or calculations checked?

### Robotics Or Engineering Project Assistant

Create an assistant that helps a team plan parts, wiring, code, tests, and troubleshooting for a robot or device.

Assessment angle:

- Are requirements and constraints clear?
- Are safety and failure modes considered?
- Are tests realistic?

### Community Problem Solver

Use AI to prototype a solution for a local school or community issue.

Examples:

- tutoring support
- event planning
- recycling
- accessibility
- student scheduling
- club communication

Assessment angle:

- Is the user need real?
- Is the proposed solution practical?
- Did students consider risks or unintended consequences?

### Agentic Workflow Challenge

Design and demonstrate a workflow where AI helps move from requirements to plan to prototype to test cases to revision.

Assessment angle:

- Did the team supervise the workflow?
- Did they verify outputs?
- Did they know when to stop or override AI?

## Suggested 3-Hour Structure

| Time | Activity | Student Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0:00-0:15 | Challenge reveal and team planning | Problem statement, users, constraints |
| 0:15-0:45 | AI exploration | Prompt/use log, options considered |
| 0:45-1:45 | Build or prototype | App, workflow, analysis, demo, or design |
| 1:45-2:15 | Test and verify | Test cases, source checks, failure notes |
| 2:15-2:40 | Revise and prepare pitch | Final version and short explanation |
| 2:40-3:00 | Judging demos | 3-minute pitch plus 2-minute questions |

## Required Student Submission

Keep the submission lightweight so judging can happen quickly.

### 1. Final Artifact

Examples:

- working app
- prototype
- slide
- workflow
- data analysis
- demo
- improved AI-generated artifact

### 2. One-Page AI Use Log

Students answer:

- What AI tools did you use?
- What did AI help with?
- What did you reject or change?
- What did you verify?
- What did AI get wrong?

### 3. Verification Evidence

Students provide:

- 3 tests run
- 1 thing that failed
- 1 improvement made
- 1 risk or limitation

### 4. Short Pitch

Students explain:

- problem
- solution
- how AI helped
- how they checked it
- what they would do next

## Fast Judging Rubric

Use 5 categories, 4 points each, for a total of 20 points.

| Category | What Judges Look For | Points |
|---|---|---:|
| Problem Framing | Clear user, goal, constraints, and success criteria | 4 |
| AI Workflow | AI used thoughtfully; process is visible; not just copied output | 4 |
| Prototype / Result | Useful, relevant, functional enough for the time available | 4 |
| Verification | Tests, fact checks, debugging, limitations, and failure awareness | 4 |
| Communication | Clear explanation, ownership, and honest discussion of AI's role | 4 |

Tie-breaker:

> Which team showed the best human judgment while using AI?

## Core Rules

AI may help generate:

- ideas
- code
- text
- tests
- designs
- explanations
- plans

Students are responsible for:

- verifying output
- editing output
- explaining output
- disclosing AI use
- defending decisions
- identifying limitations

Recommended rule statement:

> AI may help generate ideas, code, text, tests, designs, and explanations. The team is responsible for verifying, editing, explaining, and defending the final result.

## What To Assess

The main assessment items should be:

1. Did they define the problem clearly?
2. Did they use AI intentionally?
3. Did they build or produce something relevant?
4. Did they verify the result?
5. Can they explain and defend their choices?

These are also the main classroom assessment questions when students use AI.

## Judge Questions

Useful questions for quick interviews:

- What problem were you trying to solve?
- Who is the user?
- What did AI help you do?
- What did AI get wrong?
- How did you verify the result?
- What test gave you the most useful information?
- What changed between your first idea and final version?
- What would fail if this were used in the real world?
- What part of the final result do you understand best?
- What would you improve with more time?

## Recommended First Version

For a first competition, use either:

- **AI Build Sprint** if the goal is excitement, creativity, and broad participation.
- **Fix The Broken AI** if the goal is easier judging and stronger critical-thinking assessment.

The strongest hybrid may be:

> Give students a challenge plus a flawed AI starting point. Their job is to improve it, verify it, and turn it into a useful prototype.

This tests AI use, STEM thinking, workplace readiness, and communication in one event.

## Connection To Teacher Presentation

This competition mirrors the classroom challenge:

- AI makes production easier.
- Assessment must focus on thinking, verification, and ownership.
- Finished work alone is not enough evidence.
- Students need to show how they used AI and how they checked it.

The competition can become a model for AI-supported classroom projects, capstones, robotics work, and STEM camps.
