# Concise Presentation Message

Purpose: Capture the shortest useful version of the presentation so the message stays clear and does not become its own cognitive overload problem.

## One-Sentence Version

AI can increase output, but useful work still depends on requirements, testing, judgment, communication, and responsibility.

## Thirty-Second Version

"Used AI" is too vague. AI might replace thinking, support thinking, critique thinking, or help manage a workflow. The workplace needs people who can define requirements, use AI appropriately, verify results, communicate clearly, and own decisions. In STEM classes, that means shifting from final product only to visible thinking: assumptions, tests, evidence, revisions, and explanation.

## Five-Slide Version

| Slide | Message |
|---|---|
| 1. What Does "Used AI" Mean? | The role of AI matters more than the yes/no question. |
| 2. AI Is Moving To Workflows | Students need supervision, verification, and accountability. |
| 3. Workplace Need | Employers need AI judgment, not vague AI use. |
| 4. STEM Teacher Advantage | STEM already teaches models, data, assumptions, testing, and evidence. |
| 5. Assignment Shift | Finished work is not enough; require visible thinking. |

## Eight-Slide Version

1. Opening question: what could "used AI" mean?
2. AI use ladder: answer copier to agentic workflow.
3. AI abundance: output is easy; judgment is hard.
4. Workplace need: problem framing, verification, communication, accountability.
5. STEM connection: models, data, units, constraints, testing.
6. Assignment shift: product to process evidence.
7. Practical categories: AI-prohibited, AI-limited, AI-allowed.
8. Discussion: one assignment to redesign.

## The Tightest Framework

Use this repeatedly:

> Role, evidence, responsibility.

Ask:

1. What role did AI play?
2. What evidence shows the student understands?
3. Who is responsible for the final answer?

## Say More With Fewer Words

Replace long explanations with sharper pairs:

| Long Idea | Short Version |
|---|---|
| AI can help students generate answers, but those answers may not demonstrate learning. | Output is not understanding. |
| Teachers need to know how AI was used before deciding whether it helped or harmed learning. | The role of AI matters. |
| Students should provide evidence that they verified AI-generated work. | Trust requires tests. |
| AI can generate a lot of output, but business needs dependable results. | Output is not the product. |
| AI can support projects by helping students start, debug, and document. | AI lowers the starting barrier. |
| Students must still explain, defend, and own the work. | Students own the answer. |
| Teachers cannot rely only on AI detection tools. | Detection is not assessment. |
| AI creates too much information to process. | AI creates an abundance problem. |
| Students need to reduce AI output into something useful. | Control the output. |

## Cognitive Overload Point

AI can produce more words, code, options, and suggestions than students or teachers can process.

Short version:

> AI can produce faster than students can understand and faster than teachers can assess.

Implication:

Students need to learn compression:

- 3 useful points
- 1 thing AI got wrong
- 1 thing they changed
- 1 thing they verified
- 1 question that remains

## Best Closing Question

> What is one assignment you can adjust so students show their thinking, not just their output?

## Best Final Line

The goal is not to make students dependent on AI, and it is not to pretend AI is going away. The goal is to help students use powerful tools while remaining accurate, ethical, and responsible for their own thinking.
