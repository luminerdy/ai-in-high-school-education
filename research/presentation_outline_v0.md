# Presentation Outline v0

Title: AI in Education and in the Workplace

Audience: High school STEM teachers

Recommended length: 35-45 minutes plus discussion

## Core Message

AI is already changing schoolwork and workplace expectations. Students need more than access to tools: they need AI literacy, data literacy, subject knowledge, ethical reasoning, verification habits, and practice taking responsibility for AI-assisted work.

## Slide-by-Slide Draft

### 1. Title

AI in Education and in the Workplace

Speaker purpose:

Set a practical tone. This is not an AI sales pitch and not an AI panic talk. It is a preparation talk.

### 2. The Question For Teachers

Suggested slide text:

What should students be able to do when powerful AI tools are normal?

Speaker points:

- The question is not whether AI exists in students' lives.
- The question is what students should learn before they rely on it.
- STEM teachers are well positioned because STEM already teaches models, evidence, constraints, uncertainty, data, and iteration.

### 3. The "Why Now" Policy Signal

Evidence:

- White House executive order, April 23, 2025.
- Federal policy promotes AI literacy, educator training, early exposure to AI, high school AI coursework/certifications, and AI-related apprenticeships.

Speaker point:

AI literacy is now being framed as both an education issue and a workforce readiness issue.

### 4. What Federal Education Guidance Emphasizes

Evidence:

- U.S. Department of Education report, 2023.

Suggested slide text:

Human in the loop. Transparent. Explainable. Overridable. Effective. Equitable.

Speaker points:

- AI can automate decisions at scale, which raises the stakes.
- Teachers need authority to question and override AI systems.
- The Department rejects visions of AI replacing teachers.

### 5. What Is Happening In Schools

Evidence:

- RAND 2025, based on 2023-2024 school year.

Suggested slide text:

- 25% of teachers used AI for planning or teaching.
- Nearly 60% of principals used AI.
- Only 18% of principals reported school/district AI guidance.
- High-poverty schools reported less guidance.

Speaker point:

The problem is not simply adoption. The problem is uneven support.

### 6. The Guidance Gap

Evidence:

- TeachAI toolkit and policy tracker.

Suggested slide text:

No guidance leaves teachers and students guessing.

Speaker points:

- Guidance should address academic integrity, privacy, responsible use, assessment, parent communication, and teacher professional development.
- Guidance should be flexible enough to update as tools change.

### 7. A Concrete High School Model: PLTW Principles of AI

Evidence:

- PLTW course outline and resume, 2026.

Suggested slide text:

Responsible use + data + models + bias + prompt/context engineering + human-centered workflows.

Speaker points:

- PLTW treats AI as interdisciplinary, not just as prompting.
- The course includes data collection/cleaning, classifiers, supervised learning, bias/fairness, app design, automation pressure, and planning for AI mistakes.
- This is a useful model even for schools not adopting PLTW.

### 8. What Workplace Reports Are Saying

Evidence:

- WEF Future of Jobs 2025.

Suggested slide text:

Fast-growing skills: AI and big data, cybersecurity, technological literacy.

Still essential: analytical thinking, creativity, resilience, flexibility, lifelong learning, leadership.

Speaker point:

The future-ready student is both technically literate and intellectually durable.

### 9. The Workplace Shift: From Producing To Directing

Evidence:

- Microsoft Work Trend Index 2026.

Suggested slide text:

AI output is a starting point, not a final answer.

Speaker points:

- Workers are being asked to set intent, direct AI, evaluate output, and own outcomes.
- Quality control and critical thinking are becoming more important.
- This maps directly to classroom practice.

### 10. What Students Need To Learn

Suggested slide text:

Students need to:

- Ask better questions
- Understand data and models
- Check sources and evidence
- Recognize bias and limits
- Protect private information
- Document AI use
- Explain and defend final work

Speaker point:

Prompting is a small part of AI literacy. Verification and judgment are the larger part.

### 11. STEM Classrooms Are A Natural Fit

Suggested slide text:

STEM already teaches what AI users need:

- Models are simplifications.
- Data quality matters.
- Units and assumptions matter.
- Evidence beats confidence.
- Iteration improves solutions.
- Failure modes must be anticipated.

Speaker point:

AI is a perfect context for teaching why STEM thinking matters.

### 12. Risks Teachers Should Name Directly

Suggested slide text:

Academic integrity, hallucinations, bias, privacy, surveillance, overreliance, inequitable access to guidance.

Speaker points:

- Do not reduce the risk conversation to "cheating."
- The deeper risk is students outsourcing the exact thinking school is meant to develop.
- AI detectors are not a complete answer; assessment design matters.

### 13. Classroom Norm: Extend Thinking

Suggested slide text:

Use AI to extend thinking, not replace it.

Good uses:

- Generate practice
- Compare explanations
- Critique an answer
- Debug code
- Explore data
- Brainstorm constraints

Speaker point:

The same tool can support learning or bypass learning depending on assignment design.

### 14. Classroom Norm: Show Human Proof

Suggested slide text:

Require evidence of thinking:

- Prompt history
- Source checks
- Error log
- Reflection
- Revision notes
- Oral defense
- Code/data/process artifacts

Speaker point:

Students should be accountable for what they submit, even when AI helped.

### 15. Human-Only Moments

Suggested slide text:

Some moments should stay human:

- First attempt at core skills
- In-class checks
- Lab safety
- Private information
- Final evidence judgment
- Personal reflection

Speaker point:

Responsible AI use includes knowing when not to use it.

### 16. Example: Science

Activity idea:

Give students an AI-generated explanation of a lab result with two subtle errors. Students must identify the errors, cite evidence, and rewrite the explanation.

Learning target:

Evaluate AI output using scientific evidence.

### 17. Example: Math

Activity idea:

Compare three AI-generated solution paths. Students identify assumptions, unit errors, skipped steps, and the clearest valid method.

Learning target:

Reason about process, not just answer.

### 18. Example: Engineering Or CS

Activity idea:

Students use AI to brainstorm failure modes or review code. They must accept, revise, or reject each suggestion with a reason.

Learning target:

Practice quality control and technical judgment.

### 19. What Schools Need Next

Suggested slide text:

1. Shared AI-use categories
2. Teacher professional learning
3. Privacy and tool approval process
4. Assessment redesign
5. Student AI literacy progression
6. Parent communication

Speaker point:

Teachers need practical structures, not slogans.

### 20. Discussion

Prompts:

- Where should AI be allowed, limited, or prohibited in your class?
- What assignment is most vulnerable to AI misuse?
- What assignment could become better with responsible AI use?
- What do students need to prove without AI?
- What do students need to practice with AI?

## Recommended Live Demo

Use AI to generate a confident but flawed STEM explanation. Ask teachers to find:

- Unsupported claim
- Missing assumption
- Data issue
- Unit or terminology issue
- What a student would need to know to catch the error

The point:

AI literacy depends on subject knowledge.
