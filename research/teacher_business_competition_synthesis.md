# Teacher, Business, And AI Competition Synthesis

Purpose: Tie together the three main threads: classroom AI use, workplace AI quality, and student AI competitions.

## Core Thesis

The common problem is not whether AI should be used.

The common problem is whether people can use AI inside a real task while preserving quality, judgment, evidence, and accountability.

Short version:

> AI output is cheap. Verified, useful, accountable work is valuable.

This applies in three places:

- teachers need students to show learning
- businesses need experts to deliver production-quality results
- competitions need judges to assess quickly and fairly

## The Shared Evaluation Pattern

Use one pattern across all three contexts:

> Goal, constraints, AI role, evidence, ownership.

| Question | Classroom | Business | Competition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goal | What is the learning target? | What business outcome or requirement matters? | What problem is the team solving? |
| Constraints | What tools, time, sources, or AI use are allowed? | What system, security, cost, schedule, or regulatory limits apply? | What time, data, tool, and judging limits apply? |
| AI Role | Did AI tutor, brainstorm, draft, test, code, or decide? | What did agents generate, change, test, or automate? | How did the team use AI during the sprint? |
| Evidence | What proves student understanding? | What proves requirements were met? | What proves the result works? |
| Ownership | Can the student explain and defend the work? | Who owns release, risk, and maintenance? | Can the team answer judge questions? |

## Why This Is Stronger Than "Use AI"

"Use AI" is too vague for school or work.

It can mean:

- copy answer
- ask for explanation
- brainstorm
- generate code
- debug
- write tests
- summarize research
- create a workflow
- delegate an agent task
- verify a result

The important question is:

> What role did AI play in the work?

The role determines whether AI supported learning, replaced learning, improved quality, or created risk.

## Teacher Implication

Teachers should not be asked to solve AI with detection alone.

A better approach is assignment design:

- define when AI is prohibited, limited, or allowed
- require short AI-use summaries
- require verification evidence
- use oral defense or walkthroughs for important work
- ask what AI got wrong
- assess thinking, not just final output

Practical classroom rule:

> If AI is allowed, students must reduce the AI output into evidence of their own thinking.

Examples:

- 3 useful points
- 1 thing AI got wrong
- 1 thing the student changed
- 1 test or source check
- 1 remaining question or limitation

## Business Implication

Businesses should not evaluate agentic work by volume of output or speed alone.

They should evaluate:

- requirements met
- tests passed
- edge cases checked
- risks understood
- architecture fit
- security and privacy handled
- maintenance path defined
- accountable expert ownership preserved

Practical workplace rule:

> Agentic work should be judged by requirements met, tests passed, risks understood, and human ownership preserved.

This is especially important because agentic workflows can produce more artifacts than humans can manually review in detail. The answer is not to read every token. The answer is to require structured evidence mapped to requirements and risks.

## Competition Implication

A 3-hour AI competition should not reward the most polished output alone.

It should reward:

- clear problem framing
- useful AI workflow
- working or plausible prototype
- verification
- explanation
- responsible use

Recommended judging question:

> Which team showed the best human judgment while using AI?

This keeps the competition aligned with both classroom learning and workplace readiness.

## A Unified Rubric

Use this rubric for classroom projects, capstones, competitions, and even workplace demos.

| Category | What To Look For |
|---|---|
| Problem Framing | Clear user, goal, constraints, and success criteria |
| AI Workflow | AI was used intentionally and its role is clear |
| Quality Of Result | Output is useful, relevant, and appropriate for the task |
| Verification | Tests, source checks, calculations, or review evidence are provided |
| Judgment | Student/team/expert accepted, rejected, or changed AI output thoughtfully |
| Communication | The work can be explained to the intended audience |
| Responsibility | A human owns the final answer and remaining risks |

For fast judging, collapse this to five categories:

1. Problem
2. AI use
3. Result
4. Verification
5. Explanation

## What Research Supports This Direction

### AI literacy needs clearer definitions.

An integrative review of AI literacy research found that AI literacy is discussed in many different ways, including functional, critical, and sociocultural dimensions. This supports the need to be specific about what kind of AI literacy is being taught or assessed.

Presentation use:

> AI literacy cannot just mean "students used a chatbot." We need to name the skill: prompting, critique, verification, ethics, workflow, or explanation.

Source:

- Gu and Ericson, "AI Literacy in K-12 and Higher Education in the Wake of Generative AI: An Integrative Review." https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.00079

### GenAI literacy requires responsible interaction, not just tool access.

Zhang and Magerko argue that the rise of generative AI creates a need for literacy around tool selection, prompting, interaction with AI, and higher-level understanding of AI systems.

Presentation use:

> Giving students access is not the same as teaching them how to interact responsibly with AI.

Source:

- Zhang and Magerko, "Generative AI Literacy: A Comprehensive Framework for Literacy and Responsible Use." https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.19038

### Real-world AI assessment should be task-oriented.

Bogart and colleagues argue that many AI literacy assessments overemphasize abstract technical knowledge and underemphasize practical job-use skills such as interpreting outputs, selecting tools, and identifying ethical concerns. Their work-task-oriented model used robotics training and a competition scenario task to assess applied AI literacy.

Presentation use:

> If we want workplace-ready AI skills, we should assess students in realistic tasks, not only vocabulary or definitions.

Source:

- Bogart et al., "AI Literacy Assessment Revisited: A Task-Oriented Approach Aligned with Real-world Occupations." https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.05475

### AI risk management is operational, not just philosophical.

NIST's AI Risk Management Framework is designed to help organizations incorporate trustworthiness into the design, development, use, and evaluation of AI systems. NIST's Generative AI Profile focuses on risks unique to generative AI and actions organizations can take.

Presentation use:

> Responsible AI is not just an ethics poster. It is requirements, evaluation, risk management, monitoring, and human accountability.

Source:

- NIST, "AI Risk Management Framework." https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework

## Slide-Ready Version

Possible slide title:

**Same Problem, Three Settings**

| Setting | Risk | Better Response |
|---|---|---|
| Classroom | Finished work may hide learning | Require visible thinking |
| Business | Agent output may hide weak requirements or testing | Require evidence mapped to requirements |
| Competition | Polished demos may hide shallow judgment | Judge problem framing, AI use, verification, and explanation |

Speaker line:

> The skill we want is not "make AI produce something." The skill is using AI to produce work that can be tested, explained, trusted, and owned.

## Recommended Message For Teachers

Use this:

> I am not asking schools to become less rigorous. I am arguing the opposite. AI means we need more explicit rigor: clearer requirements, better evidence, better tests, and better explanations.

Then connect it to STEM:

> STEM teachers already teach the habits that make AI use trustworthy: assumptions, models, data, constraints, testing, units, debugging, and explanation.

## Recommended Message For Business

Use this:

> Agents can accelerate work, but they do not remove the need for systems engineering. Requirements, interfaces, tests, risk controls, monitoring, and ownership still determine whether the work is production quality.

## Recommended Message For Competitions

Use this:

> The winning team should not be the team with the prettiest AI-generated output. It should be the team that best defines the problem, uses AI intentionally, verifies the result, and explains the tradeoffs.

## Best One-Line Summary

> The common skill is disciplined AI use: define the goal, constrain the tool, verify the result, explain the decision, and own the outcome.
