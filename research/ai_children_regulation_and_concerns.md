# AI For Children: Regulation And Concerns

Date reviewed: June 15, 2026

Purpose: Notes for the June 22 teacher presentation and discussion.

## Short Version

The U.S. does not yet have one comprehensive federal law regulating AI use by children. Instead, child AI safety is governed by a patchwork:

- COPPA for children under 13 and online data collection
- FERPA and school/district privacy obligations for student education records
- FTC consumer protection authority over unfair or deceptive practices
- State privacy, AI, deepfake, and child-safety laws
- School district acceptable-use, academic-integrity, and vendor-approval policies
- Emerging chatbot-specific laws, especially around AI companions and minors

For teachers, the practical point is not "we must solve regulation." It is:

Schools need clear local guardrails for privacy, safety, disclosure, learning, and accountability.

## How To Say This To Teachers

Suggested framing:

"AI regulation for children is still catching up. That means schools cannot wait for perfect national rules. We need practical guardrails now: approved tools, privacy rules, disclosure expectations, adult oversight, and clear boundaries around what AI should not do with students."

## Main Concerns To Address

### 1. Privacy And Student Data

Concern:

Students may enter personal, family, health, disciplinary, or educational information into general AI tools. Those tools may store, train on, or expose data depending on their terms and settings.

Teacher message:

Do not put private student information into unapproved AI tools. Use school-approved platforms and de-identified examples.

Discussion question:

What information should students and teachers never put into an AI tool?

### 2. Age Appropriateness

Concern:

Many consumer AI tools were not designed for children, and age-gating is imperfect. COPPA mainly focuses on children under 13, but high school students still need protection and guidance.

Teacher message:

High school access does not mean unrestricted access. Age, maturity, purpose, and supervision matter.

Discussion question:

Which AI uses are appropriate for middle school, early high school, and upper high school?

### 3. AI Companions And Emotional Dependence

Concern:

AI companion chatbots can simulate friendship, romance, mentorship, or emotional support. This raises risks around overreliance, self-harm responses, sexual content, manipulation, and blurred human-machine boundaries.

Teacher message:

There is a difference between AI as a learning tool and AI as a companion. Schools should be cautious about tools designed for emotional attachment.

Discussion question:

Should schools treat AI tutors and AI companions differently?

### 4. Academic Integrity

Concern:

Students may use AI to produce final answers, essays, code, lab reports, or presentations without doing the underlying thinking.

Teacher message:

The better question is not only "Did you use AI?" It is "What role did AI play, what thinking did you do, and how did you verify the result?"

Discussion question:

What AI uses should be allowed, limited, or prohibited in your class?

### 5. Accuracy And Overtrust

Concern:

GenAI can produce confident but incorrect answers. Agentic AI can also test or verify outputs, but automated verification is only as good as the criteria, tools, data, and assumptions behind it.

Teacher message:

AI does not eliminate verification. It makes verification design a core skill.

Discussion question:

What evidence should students provide to show that an AI-assisted answer is reliable?

### 6. Bias And Fairness

Concern:

AI systems may reflect bias in training data, design choices, and deployment context. This matters in grading, discipline, hiring simulations, recommendation systems, tutoring, and student support.

Teacher message:

Students need to learn that AI output is not neutral just because it is machine-generated.

Discussion question:

Where could AI bias show up in a school or STEM project?

### 7. Transparency And Disclosure

Concern:

If AI use is invisible, teachers cannot tell whether a student used AI as a tutor, editor, answer engine, or producer of the final work.

Teacher message:

Disclosure should be normal, not treated only as a confession. Students should document AI use the way they document sources, tools, data, or collaborators.

Discussion question:

What should an AI-use disclosure look like for your assignments?

### 8. Equity

Concern:

Students have unequal access to tools, paid models, adult guidance, and AI literacy. Schools with less guidance may face more confusion, misuse, or restriction.

Teacher message:

Equity is not just access to AI. It is access to good guidance on how to use AI well.

Discussion question:

How do we avoid creating a gap between students who know how to use AI productively and students who only experience AI as something banned or punished?

## Regulation: What Is Already Relevant

### COPPA

COPPA regulates online collection of personal information from children under 13. It is important for younger students and for tools that knowingly collect data from children. It does not solve the full high school AI question because most high school students are 14-18.

### FERPA

FERPA protects student education records. AI concerns arise when teachers or schools enter identifiable student information, grades, behavior records, accommodations, or other education records into third-party tools.

### FTC Consumer Protection

The FTC can investigate unfair or deceptive practices. In 2025, the FTC opened an inquiry into AI companion chatbots and their effects on children and teens, requesting information from major AI companies.

### State Laws

States are moving faster than the federal government in some areas. Examples include:

- State AI governance laws
- Deepfake and nonconsensual intimate image laws
- AI companion chatbot safety laws
- Age-assurance or online-safety laws
- School AI guidance

The details vary widely by state.

### School And District Policy

For teachers, district policy may matter more day to day than federal AI legislation. Schools need guidance on:

- approved AI tools
- student accounts
- parent consent
- data privacy
- assignment disclosure
- academic integrity
- teacher use of AI with student data
- assessment practices

## Slide Idea

Title: Regulation Is Catching Up. Classrooms Need Guardrails Now.

Bullets:

- The U.S. has a patchwork: COPPA, FERPA, FTC, state laws, district policy.
- The highest-risk areas are privacy, companion chatbots, deepfakes, bias, and overtrust.
- Teachers do not need to become lawyers.
- Students need clear rules: approved tools, no private data, disclose AI use, verify claims, human accountability.

## Suggested Discussion Prompt

"What should our local AI guardrails be before we ask students to use these tools?"

Categories:

- What tools are approved?
- What data is off-limits?
- What uses are allowed?
- What uses require disclosure?
- What uses are prohibited?
- What learning evidence must remain human?
