# Gap Analysis: STEM Teacher Presentation

Purpose: Review the current research and planning files, identify missing or underweighted points, and clarify what should be emphasized in the teacher discussion.

## Current Strengths

The existing materials are strong in these areas:

- Clear central thesis: AI makes production easier and evaluation harder.
- Practical teacher lens: focus on what STEM teachers can control rather than broad "schools should" statements.
- AI-use ladder: copy answer, Q&A, prompt engineering, tutoring, critique, workflow, agentic workflow.
- Workplace connection: employers need judgment, verification, communication, and accountability, not vague "AI use."
- Assessment shift: final products are weaker evidence of learning; process, explanation, testing, and defense matter more.
- AI detection stance: detectors may be a signal, but not reliable proof.
- Capstone/project angle: AI is useful for setup, troubleshooting, planning, and documentation if students still own the work.
- Guardrails: privacy, disclosure, approved tools, human-only moments, verification, and age-appropriate use.

## Key Points That Were Missing Or Underweighted

### 1. Teacher Workflow Use

The presentation has focused heavily on student use. Teachers may also want practical examples of AI for their own work.

Include briefly:

- generate practice problems
- make rubrics
- create differentiated explanations
- draft parent communications
- generate examples/non-examples
- brainstorm labs/projects
- adapt reading level
- create formative checks

Boundary:

Do not enter private student data into unapproved tools.

Why it matters:

Teacher use is often the lowest-risk entry point and can help teachers understand the tool before assigning it to students.

### 2. Developmental Differences: Middle School vs High School

The session is mainly high school, but middle school may come up.

High school can handle:

- disclosure
- verification
- prompt iteration
- AI critique
- project workflows
- capstone support
- ethical tradeoffs

Middle school should emphasize:

- what AI is and is not
- AI can be wrong
- do not share private information
- ask adults before using new tools
- compare AI answers with evidence
- human relationships and judgment matter

Why it matters:

AI companion and emotional-dependence risks are more serious for younger students and should not be treated the same as AI-assisted capstone work.

### 3. Tool Access And Equity

AI access is uneven:

- free vs paid models
- blocked vs allowed tools
- students with AI-literate adults at home vs students without guidance
- students with stronger writing skills may use AI differently from students who are struggling
- students may be falsely accused at different rates

Recommended point:

Equity is not only access to AI tools. It is access to good guidance, safe tools, and fair assessment.

### 4. Local Policy And Approved Tools

The materials mention policy generally but do not know the local district/state rules.

Do not pretend to know local policy unless verified.

Recommended language:

"I do not want to make assumptions about your local approved tools or policy. The framework here is meant to help you ask better questions: what is approved, what data is off-limits, what uses require disclosure, and what evidence of learning is needed?"

### 5. AI Companions vs AI Learning Tools

This deserves a short distinction if concerns arise.

AI learning tool:

- helps explain, practice, brainstorm, or critique schoolwork
- should be bounded by assignment goals

AI companion:

- simulates friendship, emotional support, romance, or personal advice
- creates different youth-safety risks

Recommended point:

Do not collapse these into one category. A chatbot used for practice questions is not the same risk profile as a companion bot designed for emotional attachment.

### 6. Multimodal And Agentic AI

The outline discusses agentic workflow, but teachers may not realize AI is no longer only text.

Include one sentence:

AI is increasingly multimodal: text, code, images, audio, video, data, screen actions, and tool use.

Why it matters:

Detection and assignment design get harder when AI can produce diagrams, code, slides, images, spreadsheets, and videos.

### 7. Student Metacognition

We have mentioned metacognition, but it should be more visible.

Useful student question:

"Do I understand this well enough to explain it without AI?"

Why it matters:

AI can create the feeling of understanding. Teachers need students to test whether they actually understand.

### 8. Specific Evidence Menus By STEM Area

The materials list general evidence types. Teachers may benefit from discipline examples.

Examples:

- Math: unit checks, alternate solution explanation, error analysis, in-class transfer problem.
- Science: lab notebook, claim-evidence-reasoning, raw data, uncertainty/errors, source validation.
- Engineering: requirements, constraints, tradeoff matrix, test plan, failure modes.
- CS/Robotics: code walkthrough, test cases, debugging log, sensor readings, rejected AI suggestions.

### 9. "What Not To Cover"

The current materials are broad. For a 60-minute session, avoid:

- long history of AI
- detailed law/regulation lecture
- deep technical explanation of LLM training
- too many statistics
- speculative job-loss claims
- tool-by-tool comparison
- centering AI detectors as the solution
- unverified breaking-news examples

## Recommended Session Priority

Use this order:

1. AI use is not one thing.
2. Workplace needs AI judgment, not vague tool use.
3. Student assignments need visible thinking.
4. Detection is insufficient; design better evidence.
5. Capstones/projects are a strong constructive use case.
6. Teachers can start with allowed/limited/prohibited categories.
7. Discussion: apply the framework to one real assignment.

## Core Message To Keep Repeating

The question is not "Did you use AI?"

The better question is:

"What role did AI play, what thinking did the student do, and what evidence shows the result is reliable?"
