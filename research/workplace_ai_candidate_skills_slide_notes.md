# Workplace AI Candidate Skills: Slide Notes

Purpose: Help explain what employers need from job candidates in an AI-enabled workplace.

## Core Point

Do not frame workplace AI readiness as "knows ChatGPT" or "uses AI." That is too vague.

Frame it as:

Can the candidate use AI inside a real workflow while protecting quality, privacy, accuracy, and accountability?

## Suggested Slide Title

What Employers Need Is Not "AI Use." It Is AI Judgment.

## Candidate Skill Grid

| Candidate Capability | What It Looks Like In A Workplace | High School Translation |
|---|---|---|
| Problem framing | Defines the task, audience, constraints, and success criteria before using AI | Students state the goal, assumptions, and what a good answer must include |
| Clear communication | Gives AI enough context and writes clearly for humans | Students write precise prompts, explanations, and reflections |
| Domain knowledge | Knows enough to spot weak or false AI output | Students understand the math, science, code, or engineering concept |
| Verification | Checks AI output against sources, data, calculations, tests, or requirements | Students cite evidence, run test cases, check units, compare sources |
| Revision judgment | Accepts, rejects, or modifies AI suggestions intentionally | Students explain what they changed and why |
| Privacy and safety | Does not paste sensitive company/customer/student data into unapproved tools | Students learn what information should never go into AI |
| Accountability | Owns the final answer instead of blaming the tool | Students defend final work orally or in writing |
| Collaboration | Shares AI workflows and learns from others instead of hiding usage | Students document prompts, process, and decisions |
| Ethical reasoning | Considers fairness, bias, harm, and appropriate human oversight | Students identify who could be affected by an AI-supported decision |
| Adaptability | Learns new tools without depending on one model or interface | Students practice transferable AI literacy, not tool loyalty |

## Workplace Struggles To Name

Employers are struggling with:

- Inconsistent AI use across employees
- Confident but wrong AI output
- Data leakage and privacy risk
- Candidates who can produce polished output but cannot explain it
- Overreliance on AI without domain understanding
- Weak documentation of how AI was used
- Unclear accountability when AI contributes to work
- Agentic systems that can act across steps but need guardrails and evaluation

## Interview-Style Assessment

Instead of asking:

"Do you know how to use AI?"

Ask:

"Show me how you would use AI to solve this work problem, and show me how you would verify the result."

Example task:

"Here is a messy customer complaint, a small dataset, or a technical issue. Use AI if you want. Produce a recommendation, explain what AI helped with, identify what you checked, and name remaining risks."

What to look for:

- Did the candidate define the problem before prompting?
- Did they provide context and constraints?
- Did they challenge the AI output?
- Did they verify claims?
- Did they protect sensitive data?
- Did they explain tradeoffs clearly?
- Did they take responsibility for the final recommendation?

## High School Classroom Connection

Students should practice AI-supported work in ways that mirror good workplace behavior:

- Use AI to brainstorm, critique, debug, or revise
- Keep a simple AI-use log
- Verify against evidence
- Show rejected AI suggestions
- Explain final reasoning
- Defend work with questions
- Avoid private data

## Useful Line For Presentation

The workplace does not need students who can make AI produce a nice answer. It needs people who can define the problem, use AI appropriately, verify the result, communicate clearly, and own the decision.

## Possible Discussion Prompt For Teachers

"If you were hiring a student intern for a STEM workplace, what would you want them to be able to prove about their AI use?"

Follow-up:

"Where in your class could students practice that proof?"
