# Monday Slide Draft

Purpose: Short slide draft for the STEM teacher discussion on Monday, June 22, 2026.

Recommended use: 12-18 minutes of presentation, then discussion.

## Slide 1: AI In Education And Work

Main point:

> AI can help with the work. Humans own the judgment.

Speaker notes:

- This is not mainly a technology talk.
- This is a learning, work, and judgment talk.
- The goal is not to decide whether AI is good or bad.
- The goal is to decide what students must still understand, verify, and own.

## Slide 2: The Problem Is Old. The Scale Is New.

Main point:

> AI did not create the learning problem. It exposed and accelerated it.

Speaker notes:

- Students have always been able to get too much help.
- Students have always been able to write many words without deep understanding.
- Students have always been able to avoid hard thinking.
- AI changes the speed, polish, scale, and availability.

Key line:

> The challenge is old. The tool is new.

## Slide 3: "Used AI" Is Too Vague

Main point:

> The role of AI matters.

Use the ladder:

| Level | AI Role | Learning Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Copy answer | Replaces thinking |
| 1 | Q&A | May help or bypass |
| 2 | Prompt/context engineering | Student directs the tool |
| 3 | Tutor | Student practices with support |
| 4 | Critic/reviewer | Student revises and evaluates |
| 5 | Workflow assistant | Student manages process |
| 6 | Agentic workflow | AI plans/tests/revises under guardrails |

Discussion prompt:

> When someone says a student used AI, what could that mean?

## Slide 4: What Work Needs

Main point:

> Output is not the product. Verified results are.

Work needs:

- clear requirements
- domain knowledge
- systems thinking
- testing
- verification
- communication
- ownership

Speaker notes:

- AI can speed up search, synthesis, drafts, code, and test ideas.
- Business still needs dependable results.
- Agents can help create work, but experts still own requirements, risk, release, and quality.

Key line:

> AI can increase output, but useful work still depends on requirements, testing, judgment, communication, and responsibility.

## Slide 5: STEM Teachers Already Teach The Needed Habits

Main point:

> AI makes STEM thinking more important, not less.

STEM habits:

- models are simplifications
- data quality matters
- assumptions matter
- units matter
- constraints shape design
- tests reveal failure
- evidence beats confidence
- code must be explained and verified

Speaker notes:

- These are also workplace AI skills.
- This is why STEM teachers are central to AI readiness.

## Slide 6: Finished Work Is Not Enough

Main point:

> Assessment shifts from product only to evidence of thinking.

When AI is allowed, ask students to show:

- goal, constraints, and assumptions
- AI-use summary
- sources, calculations, tests, or unit checks
- what AI got wrong
- what the student changed
- remaining risk or limitation
- short explanation or oral defense

Key line:

> Use AI to reduce unnecessary friction, not necessary thinking.

## Slide 7: A Practical Way To Start

Main point:

> Pick one assignment and define the AI role.

Use three categories:

| Category | Use When | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| AI-Prohibited | independent skill practice or assessment | no AI |
| AI-Limited | AI supports but does not replace learning | disclose use |
| AI-Allowed | AI workflow literacy is part of the goal | process evidence and verification |

Discussion prompt:

> Pick one assignment or project. How could AI help? How could AI break the learning? What evidence would prove the student still understands?

## Optional Slide: Teachers Need Hands-On Experience

Main point:

> You cannot evaluate AI well from the sidelines.

Speaker notes:

- There is a lot of talk, fear, and policy discussion.
- That matters, but it is not enough.
- Teachers need to roll up their sleeves and use AI on one or two real projects.
- Until you try a real workflow, it is hard to understand what AI can do, where it fails, and what students will experience.

Suggested teacher project:

- redesign one assignment
- plan a small lab or project
- build a simple app or data workflow
- create test cases for a lesson
- use AI to critique student-facing instructions
- document what helped, what failed, and what still required human judgment

Key line:

> Guided student use starts with guided teacher experience.

## Optional Slide: Projects, Capstones, And AI Quest

Main point:

> AI can lower the barrier to starting. It should not lower the requirement to understand.

AI can help with:

- idea scope
- requirements
- setup
- debugging
- documentation
- test planning
- presentation structure

Students still own:

- design choices
- safety
- verification
- prototype evidence
- final explanation

## Closing Question

> What is one assignment or project you can adjust so students show their thinking, not just their output?

## Best Final Line

The goal is not to make students dependent on AI, and it is not to pretend AI is going away. The goal is to help students use powerful tools while remaining accurate, ethical, and responsible for their own thinking.
